Philosophy of Islamic Finance

What is Finance?

Finance concerns resource allocation, management, acquisition, and investment. It is the flow of funds through the financial system, which can be both direct and indirect.

The Islamic Worldview

The Islamic financial system is based on a specific worldview that differs from conventional finance:

Istikhlaf (Vicegerency)

This principle implies that man is not the absolute owner of resources, but rather he is entrusted with them and may utilize them only for the betterment of mankind. Therefore, man is accountable to Allah for his conduct on earth.

The concept of trust, in Islam, is linked with responsibility, implying that wealth that is entrusted upon a human being is indeed a responsibility that he will be accounted for in the Hereafter.

What is Islamic Finance?

Islamic Finance comprises financial markets and institutions that deal with resource allocation, management, acquisition, and investment in accordance with Shariah principles. It is the provision of financial services on a basis that is compliant with the principles and rules of Islamic commercial jurisprudence (Fiqh al-Mua'malat).

Principles of Islamic Finance

Islam promotes economically productive activities and genuine trade and business transactions on one side; while it calls for avoidance of interest-based transactions, no involvement in illegal and unethical activities and avoidance of speculative transactions, on the other.

Validity versus Permissibility

Validity (Sah)

  • Based on the FORM of the contract
  • A valid contract is not necessarily permissible

Permissibility (Halal)

  • Based on the SUBSTANCE of the contract
  • And the INTENTION of the contractors

A financial contract must be both halal (permissible) and sah (valid)

The concept of 'iwad (Equal Countervalue)

Islam prohibits riba but permits sale (bay'). The element of 'iwad promotes a sense of equity and justice in economic transactions, differentiating it from riba.

"Every increase, which is without 'iwad or equal countervalue, is riba" – Ibn al-'Arabi

Shariah Framework for Islamic Finance

What is Shariah?

Shariah literally means 'path to watering place'. In technical terms, it refers to the path to tread for guidance in this world.

It encompasses the commands, prohibitions, and values prescribed by Allah for His slaves either through Al-Quran or As-Sunnah (a divinely guided lifestyle and whatever was reported about what the Prophet Muhammad (pbuh) said, did, or gave his tacit approval).

According to one observer, the Sharī'ah is the "epitome of Islamic thought, the most typical manifestation of the Islamic way of life, the core and kernel of Islam itself".

Riba (Interest/Usury)

What is Riba?

Riba literally means excess, increase, expansion, or growth. In Shariah, it refers to:

  • A surplus of commodity or an excess in return without counter value (Hanafi school)
  • A predetermined excess or surplus over and above the loan received by the creditor conditionally in relation to a specified time period

The Prohibition of Riba in the Qur'an

O you who believe, fear Allah and give up what remains [due to you] of interest, if you should be believers. And if you do not, then be informed of a war [against you] from Allah and His Messenger. But if you repent, you may have your principal [without interest] – [thus] you do no wrong, nor are you wronged.

(Al-Baqarah: 278-279)

Categories of Riba

Loan Riba or Riba Qard/Duyun
  • Also known as Riba al-Quran because it is prohibited by the Quran
  • Applies to loans or any debt regardless of cause
  • Two types:
    • Riba' al-qard (imposed from the beginning)
    • Riba' jahiliyyah (only imposed after default)
Sales Riba or Riba al-Buyun
  • Also known as Riba as-Sunnah because it is prohibited by the Sunnah
  • Applies to sale transactions in certain commodities
  • Two types:
    • Riba al nasi'ah (delay in paying or delivery)
    • Riba al fadl (exchanging one ribawi commodity for the same commodity but unequal in amount)

Hadith on Ribawi Items

"Gold for gold, silver for silver, wheat for wheat, barley for barley, dates for dates, salt for salt – like for like, equal for equal, and hand-to-hand (spot); if the commodities differ, then you may sell as you wish, provided that the exchange is hand-to-hand or a spot transaction."

Why is Interest Prohibited?

  • It is an injustice to borrowers who must repay regardless of their financial situation
  • It is unfair to lenders when returns on investments are much higher than interest charged
  • Riba corrupts society (Surah ar-Rum: 37-41)
  • Riba implies improper appropriation of others' property (Surah an-Nisa': 160-161)
  • Riba's ultimate effect is negative growth (Surah al-Baqarah: 276)
  • Riba demeans and diminishes human personality (Surah al-Baqarah: 275)

Gharar (Excessive Uncertainty)

What is Gharar?

Literal meaning: Deceit, fraud, uncertainty, danger, peril, or hazard that might lead to destruction or loss.

Technical meaning: Uncertainty and/or ignorance of one or both parties in a contract over the substance or attributes of the object of sale, or doubt over its existence and availability at the time of contract.

Types of Gharar

Gharar Yasir (Minor Gharar)

Contracts involving minor gharar are permissible and valid.

Gharar Fahish (Major Gharar)

Uncertainty is so great that it becomes unacceptable. Related to factors such as buyer and seller, asset, price, and contract.

Classical Examples of Gharar Sale

  • Sale of fish still in the sea
  • Sale of birds in the air
  • Sale of unborn animals

Gharar in Modern Financial Transactions

  • Financing documentation must feature sufficient details to avoid gharar
  • Conventional insurance is deemed not Shariah-compliant because it entails gharar
  • The Islamic alternative to conventional insurance is takaful, based on mutual assistance

Maysir (Gambling)

What is Maysir?

Gambling or maysir, also known as qimar, is defined as any activity which involves betting whereby the winner will take the entire bet and the loser will lose his bet. It means games of pure chance where any party might gain at the expense of the loss of the other party.

Prohibition of Gambling in the Qur'an

"O believers, wine and gambling (maysir), idols and divination by arrows are but abominations devised by the Satan, avoid them so that you may prosper. Indeed Satan seeks to stir up enmity and hatred among you by means of wine and gambling and to keep you away from remembrance of Allah swt and from your prayers."

(Surah al-Maidah, verses 90-91)

Why is Gambling Prohibited?

  • Gambling does not create wealth; it only redistributes what already exists
  • The betting is done on the basis of other people's wealth
  • Because speculation resembles gambling, it increases volatility in financial markets
  • It invokes enmity in society and distracts believers from worshipping Allah

Differences Between Islamic Finance and Conventional Finance

In the Islamic financial system, money is not a commodity as is the case with the conventional financial system. Islamic finance is community-oriented and entrepreneur-friendly, emphasizing productivity and physical expansion of economic production and services.

Key Differences

Conventional Finance Islamic Finance
Man-made principles Principles prescribed by Islamic law
Does not need an asset to be purchased for transaction to happen Promotes purchase of an asset to generate an income or a return
Charging of interest permitted Prohibits charging of interest
Structures do not need to be approved for compliance Structures approved by Shari'ah Advisory Committee or Shari'ah Supervisory Board
Prohibited activities depends on each bank's frameworks Financing of certain activities such as gambling is not permitted

Development of Islamic Finance in Malaysia

Journey of Islamic Finance

1

1960s - 1970s: Early Foundations

  • 1963: Mit Ghamr Local Savings Bank (Egypt) and Lembaga Tabung Haji (Malaysia)
  • 1971: Nasser Social Bank (Egypt)
  • 1975: Islamic Development Bank (Jeddah) and Dubai Islamic Bank
  • 1977: Faisal Islamic Bank (Sudan)
  • 1979: Bahrain Islamic Bank
2

1980s: Expansion Phase

  • 1982: Al-Baraka
  • 1983: Bank Islam Malaysia Berhad
  • 1983: Islamic Bank, Bangladesh
  • 1984: Dar al-Maal Islamic Trust, Geneva
  • 1989: ANZ Global Islamic Finance, UK
3

1990s: Institutional Development

  • 1991: Accounting & Auditing Organization for Islamic Financial Institutions (AAOIFI), Bahrain
  • 1993: Islamic Bank of Brunei
  • 1994: Islamic Inter-banks Money Market, Malaysia
  • 1999: Bank Muamalat Malaysia Berhad
4

2000s and Beyond: Global Integration

  • 2002: International Islamic Financial Market, Bahrain and Islamic Financial Services Board, Kuala Lumpur
  • 2006: International Centre for Education in Islamic Finance (INCEIF)
  • 2010: International Islamic Liquidity Management Corporation (IILM)

Malaysia's Development Approach

Inception Stage (1983-1993)

Instituting foundations of Islamic finance and financial inclusion. Building the foundation of legal, regulatory and Shari'ah framework.

Intermediate Stage (1993-2001)

Institutional building, stimulate competition, activity generation and market vibrancy. Increase the number of players, develop financial markets and enhance Shari'ah governance.

Advanced Stage (2001-Present)

Vision creation, comprehensive and international integration. Market liberalization, upgrade of infrastructures, and strengthening the overall Islamic financial landscape.

Implementation of Islamic Finance in Malaysia

Phase 1 (1970s - 1980s)

Establishment of Islamic financial institutions:

  • Tabung Haji
  • Bank Islam Malaysia Berhad
  • Syarikat Takaful Malaysia Berhad

Phase 2 (1980s - 1990s)

Conventional banks were allowed to offer Islamic financial products and services under Islamic Banking Scheme:

  • Interest-free Banking Scheme

Phase 3 & 4 (1990s - 2000s)

Conventional banks were allowed to set up Islamic subsidiaries, and international integration of the domestic Islamic banking system:

  • Kuwait Finance House (KFH)
  • Al-Rajhi Bank
  • Asian Finance Bank (AFB)

The Current Scenario

  • The global Islamic finance market is growing moderately, due to strong investments in the Halal Sectors, infrastructure, and Sukuk bonds
  • The industry's total worth was estimated to be USD 2.05 trillion in 2017, marking an 8.3% growth in assets
  • Global SUKUK outstanding surged by a record 25.6% to close at USD 399.9 billion as at end 2017
  • Islamic banking is the largest sector in the Islamic finance industry, contributing to 71%, or USD 1.72 trillion, of the industry's assets

Key Market Trends

Islamic Banking is the Largest Segment

  • There were 505 Islamic banks in 2017, including 207 Islamic Banking windows
  • A growing number of digital-only, or 'disruptor banks' with no physical branches, have emerged
  • Islamic banks are catching up with the launch of digital-only subsidiaries
  • Islamic banking is commonly seen to have advantages in terms of moral standards and ties to tangible assets

GCC Regional Overview

  • In the GCC, the market share of Islamic banking crossed the 25% threshold
  • GCC Islamic banking assets reached USD 490 billion by the end of June 2013
  • Saudi Arabia dominates the region with a 49% share
  • Islamic banking has acquired systemic proportions in Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, and the United Arab Emirates

Competitive Landscape

  • The global Islamic finance market is fragmented with many players trying to grab a significant chunk of the developing market
  • In some regions, like Asia and Africa, it is moderately growing with the presence of a large number of local players and some major players
  • GCC is a highly competitive market, with the presence of large number of international players
  • Bank Al-Rajhi, Dubai Islamic Bank, and Kuwait House Finance, are among the major players present in the region

Opportunities and Challenges

Opportunities

  • Growing Muslim population and rising awareness about Islamic finance
  • Increasing government support in many countries
  • Expansion into new markets and sectors
  • Technological innovations in Islamic fintech
  • Growth in Islamic social finance (zakat, waqf, etc.)

Challenges

  • Lack of standardization across different jurisdictions
  • Limited awareness and understanding of Islamic finance
  • Competition from conventional finance institutions
  • Need for more innovation in product development
  • Regulatory challenges and differences across countries
